1919-1930s: Early Urban Planning and Garden City Movement

The government held an urban planning conference in Wellington in 1919 to avoid the bad environmental influences marginal areas of Britain had already seen. From the meeting, there were two leading solutions: either to move urban people to new towns or reform the existing cities. The general assumption was that by doing proper town planning, proper towns or cities could be made healthy with the contribution of social stability. The second solution, the Garden Suburbs model, was more successful, with an early prototype developed in London's Hampstead. Architect Reginald Hammond tried to follow this model in Durie Hill but did not complete the project.

1940s-1950s: State Housing and Suburban Sprawl

The State Housing Program arose out of the belief that town planning could significantly remake urban life, especially following the ruin of two world wars. This program drove the building of new state housing suburbs, notably in the Hutt Valley with Epuni, Naenae, and Taita, based on the garden city concept. However, these towns were not very socially successful, as residents preferred staying at home or socializing with neighbors rather than using community centers. As towns expanded, urban planning faced challenges due to space and resource limitations. This often led councils to rezone rural land for urban use, resulting in the loss of productive rural land and high infrastructure costs. Solutions included green belts around cities and denser settlements. Rapid suburban growth undermined the 1949 Auckland Development Plan's greenbelt restrictions.

1950s-1970s: Motorway Construction and Urban Renewal

By the early 1950s, urban growth and increased car ownership necessitated an extensive road network. Auckland planners initially proposed suburban railways but opted for a road-based system due to the city's low population density. The Auckland Harbour Bridge, opened in 1959, improved connectivity between Auckland and the North Shore. Urban renewal followed the impact of motorways on older neighborhoods, leading to gentrification as families bought and renovated old slum houses and apartments. This revitalized certain areas but displaced poorer populations to the outskirts.

1980s-1990s: Deregulation and the Resource Management Act

In 1984, the Labour government believed that old-style regulation hindered economic growth and started a deregulation policy, strengthening private property rights and easing building restrictions. The Resource Management Act (RMA) of 1991 replaced the Town and Country Planning Act, further relaxing regulations. This led to the replacement of historic buildings with skyscrapers and the encroachment of shopping centers and business parks into residential areas, highlighting the need for coordinated urban planning.

2000 to 2017: Coordinated Growth Strategies

Since the early 2000s, local bodies have devised more policies for coordinated growth to control urban development. The 1999 Auckland Regional Growth Strategy set out a 50-year vision to upgrade the suburban train network and restrain sprawl through urban consolidation.
